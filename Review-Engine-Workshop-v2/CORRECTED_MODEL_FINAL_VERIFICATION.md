## Labor Cost Handling - Detailed Explanation

### Why Labor Cost Matters
In the old model, I was implicitly claiming:
- Year 1 impact: -$68.3K from labor reduction
- PLUS: +$83.8K from revenue growth  
- EQUALS: +$141.2K combined

But this violates basic financial logic:
- If you reduce labor cost by $68.3K (cutting headcount from 2.02 to 0.76 FTE), you're left with 0.76 FTE
- Those remaining 0.76 FTE can't also do the RevOps work that generates +$83.8K in revenue
- The RevOps work requires the freed 26.25 hours, which come from the team that's still on payroll

### The Correct Model
- **Option A (Cost reduction):** Cut team from 2.02 to 0.76 FTE → save $68.3K labor → $207.9K profit (no growth)
- **Option B (Internal reallocation):** Keep team at 2.02 FTE ($109.2K) → reallocate 26.25 hrs to RevOps → generate $83.8K new revenue → $210.8K profit (with growth)

The profit difference between the two scenarios is only +$3K ($71.2K vs $68.3K), but the strategic implications are very different.

## Implementation Path

### Recommended: Pursue Option B (Internal Reallocation)

**Advantages:**
1. No severance costs for team members
2. Revenue growth ($83.8K annually) vs pure cost cutting
3. Sustainable business model - growth compounds
4. Employee morale - modern tools + high-value work
5. Competitive advantage - customer success + product innovation drive market position
6. Flexibility - if RevOps doesn't work, can always pursue cost reduction later

**Risk Mitigation:**
- Set clear RevOps targets by month 6 (revenue growth indicators)
- If targets not met by month 9, transition to cost-reduction scenario
- Detailed tracking of freed hours usage
